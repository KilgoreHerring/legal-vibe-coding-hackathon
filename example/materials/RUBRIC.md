# Marking Rubric — Fairway Events v Luxe Marquees

**Total marks:** 85 (Task 1: 45 pts | Task 2: 40 pts)

---

## Overall Approach

The trainee should analyse the factual scenario methodically, starting with the contract to identify specific breaches, then matching each breach to a head of loss with a specific contractual or common law basis.

The **LBA** should be comprehensive, protocol-compliant, and professional in tone — it is a document the other side will see.

The **advice note** should be candid and balanced — it is privileged and the client has specifically asked for a realistic assessment.

The **key distinction** the trainee must make: what goes in the LBA (firm assertion of position) vs what goes in the advice note (honest assessment of strengths AND weaknesses). This distinction is itself a test of professional judgment.

---

## Key Issues

| Issue | What to look for |
|---|---|
| Late installation | Time was of the essence (Clause 2.2), clear breach |
| Non-conforming equipment | Wrong furniture and lighting, breach of Clauses 3.1/3.2 and Schedule 1 |
| Defective installation | Torn linings, sagging roof, uneven flooring |
| Liquidated damages | Clause 5.1, £2,000 per day, genuine pre-estimate vs penalty (*Makdessi*) |
| Replacement costs | Direct loss, reasonable mitigation, Clause 3.3 right to replacement |
| Remoteness | Harringtons loss is consequential, *Hadley v Baxendale* two-limb test |
| Revenue vs profit | Lost business damages on lost profit basis, not revenue |
| Mitigation | Client must take reasonable steps; replacement was reasonable |
| Deposit recovery | Repudiatory breach arguable but risky — event went ahead (affirmation) |
| Total failure of consideration | Difficult given partial performance |
| Guest who tripped | Potential OLA/negligence claim, Clause 6.1 indemnity, contribution under CLCA 1978 |
| Pre-action protocol | Delay in responding to October 2025 LBA |
| ADR | Must propose mediation, *Halsey v Milton Keynes* costs consequences |
| Track allocation | Harringtons loss pushes towards multi-track |
| Privilege | LBA must not contain strategy or weakness assessment |

## Critical Errors

These are the errors that reveal a fundamental misunderstanding of the exercise.

| Error | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Including privileged advice or strategy in the LBA | The LBA goes to the other side. Disclosing weaknesses is a serious professional error. |
| Failing to identify the Harringtons loss as a remoteness issue | Missing this shows a failure to understand how consequential loss works in contract law. |
| Claiming full £65,000 revenue as damages without addressing profit vs revenue | Damages are calculated on lost profit, not lost revenue. This is a basic error. |
| Overstating the deposit recovery claim without acknowledging the affirmation risk | The event went ahead. This is the weakest head and must be identified as such. |
| Missing the ADR proposal entirely | Protocol non-compliance with potential costs consequences. |
| Failing to reference the contract clauses | Drafting without reference to the actual agreement is a basic error. |
| Aggressive or unprofessional tone in the LBA | |
| Failing to address the delay in responding to Barker & Co's October letter | Ignoring this looks like the trainee hasn't read the facts properly. |
| Advice note is one-sided — only strengths, no weaknesses | This is the opposite of what the client asked for and shows no professional judgment. |
| Treating the LBA and advice note identically | No understanding of the privilege distinction. |

---

## Quality Markers

A strong submission will include all of the following:

**LBA:**
- Comprehensive, protocol-compliant and professionally drafted
- Each head of loss tied to a specific contractual clause or legal principle
- Summary table in the LBA clearly setting out the counterclaim
- Set-off argument clearly stated — counterclaim exceeds Luxe's claim
- ADR proposal with reference to costs consequences (*Halsey*)

**Advice Note:**
- Distinguishes between strong and weak heads of claim
- Deposit recovery honestly assessed as the weakest argument
- Harringtons loss analysed with reference to remoteness (*Hadley v Baxendale*)
- Revenue vs profit distinction made for Harringtons claim
- Best-case, realistic and worst-case scenarios with figures
- Settlement range recommended with commercial reasoning
- Guest who tripped identified with indemnity and contribution analysis
- Practical next steps requested from client
