# What You Need to Know Before We Start

Welcome to the Innovation Team's vibe coding session. You're not here to become a developer.

You're here to find out what becomes possible when you can describe an idea clearly enough for an AI to build it.

---

## What Is Vibe Coding?

Vibe coding means building a real, working piece of software by describing what you want in plain English - no programming knowledge required.

You tell an AI assistant called Claude what you want to build, what it should do, and who it's for. Claude writes the code. You test it, give feedback, and keep refining until it does what you imagined.

The skill is thinking clearly about a problem and communicating it precisely. Lawyers tend to be good at this.

---

## How Today Works

You're in a group of around 4–5 people. Each group has one facilitator from the Innovation Team - they'll handle the technical side so you can focus on the ideas.

**The session is 90 minutes.** At the end, each group presents what they built to the room in 5 minutes.

Here's the shape of the session:

| | |
|---|---|
| **First 15 minutes** | Work out what you're building and who it's for |
| **Next 5 minutes** | Plan it out and kick off the build |
| **Next 35 minutes** | Build, test, and iterate |
| **Next 20 minutes** | Polish and prepare your demo |
| **Final 15 minutes** | Present to the room |

---

## Your Job vs. the Facilitator's Job

**Your job:** Come up with the idea. Define the problem. Describe what you want. Test it. Give feedback. Tell the story at the end.

**The facilitator's job:** Type the prompts into Claude, handle any technical hiccups, and keep the build moving.

You don't need to touch a keyboard unless you want to. The thinking is the work.

---

## What Makes a Good Idea

The best ideas have three things in common. They're real: something you've actually experienced, not theoretical. They're specific: "trainees waste time chasing signature pages" is a problem, "legal processes are inefficient" isn't. And they're contained: solvable with one focused tool, not a whole platform.

The themes for today are:

- Document tasks: signature pages, redline summaries, CP lists
- Training and learning: tools that help trainees get up to speed faster
- Relationships and BD: staying close to clients, spotting opportunities
- Knowledge: making the firm's expertise easier to find and use

You don't have to stay strictly within a theme. If your group has a better idea, run with it.

Start by asking yourself: *"What's the most annoying thing I do repeatedly at work that a computer should probably be doing?"*

---

## What You'll Present at the End

Each group gets 5 minutes. You'll cover:

1. **The problem** - who has it and why it matters
2. **What you built** - what it does in one sentence, then a live demo
3. **What it would take to ship** - an honest look at what getting this into production would actually involve
4. **The ask** - if you were taking this further, what's the next step?

You'll have a presentation file generated automatically to help structure this. The demo is the centrepiece - build something you can actually show working.

---

## What You Don't Need to Worry About

- Whether you understand the code - you don't need to
- Whether your idea is too simple - simple and working beats complex and broken
- Whether it's "good enough" - prototypes are supposed to be rough; that's the point
- Whether it would actually pass an IT review today - the shipping checklist covers that at the end

---

## One Thing to Do Before We Start

Think about your last few weeks in your seat. What took longer than it should have? What did you do manually that felt like something a computer could handle? What information did you have to chase down that should have been easy to find?

That's probably your app idea.
