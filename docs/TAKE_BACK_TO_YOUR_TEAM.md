# Take it Back to Your Team

A short pack to use after the vibe coding hackathon. The point is not to turn you into a developer overnight. It is to give you something useful to bring into the next conversation with your team, your supervisor, or your practice group, so the day does not stop at the demo.

Read it on the train home. Keep it open when you have your next team catch-up.

---

## What you actually did today

In 90 minutes, with no coding background, you and your group built a working prototype of an internal tool. You did not write any of the code. You described what you wanted, looked at what came back, said what was wrong, and iterated. That is vibe coding.

The thing on the screen at the end is real. It runs in a browser. It does what you told it to do. It is not a mock-up, it is software.

That is the point you need to make first when you tell people about it. People will assume you watched a demo. You did not. You built something.

---

## How to explain it in 60 seconds

If a partner or supervisor asks you what the day was about, here is the version that lands:

> "We spent the morning using AI tools to build working prototypes of internal applications. Each group of four trainees, with one facilitator from the Innovation Team, started with a problem from a team or practice group and ended with a working web app and a five-minute pitch. None of us had coded before. The point was not to learn programming. It was to see how quickly an idea can become a thing you can click on, and to start spotting where this could be useful in our actual work."

Three things to land:
1. We built, we did not watch
2. It took 90 minutes from blank page to working tool
3. The constraint was the idea, not the technology

## What to bring into your next team conversation

Pick one or two of these depending on the team and the moment.

**The "what would you build?" question.** Ask your team what they would build if a working prototype took 90 minutes instead of 90 days. The answers tell you what they find painful. That list is gold. Write the answers down.

**The "what do we redo every time?" question.** Vibe coding is best on small, repetitive, specific problems. Standard letter generators. Bundle index drafters. First-pass document checkers. Knowledge lookups. Things people in the team build a Word template for, again and again. Look for those.

**The "what would a junior do?" question.** A lot of trainee work is process-heavy: extracting points from documents, summarising, comparing, reformatting, drafting standard language. These are exactly the tasks where a 90-minute prototype can save someone a half-day a week. Spot them.

**The honest version of the day.** Tell them what worked and what did not. The prototype probably broke at some point. You probably had to rephrase something five times. That is part of the story. Do not oversell.

## What to say when someone asks "should we use this for client work?"

Short answer: not yet, and not from your laptop.

Longer answer: what you built today runs in a browser, was made in 90 minutes, and has not been through any security review. That is fine for a prototype. It is not fine for client data. The path from "prototype" to "thing the firm uses" goes through the Innovation Team, IT, InfoSec, and a data classification check. See `SHIPPING_EXAMPLE.md` for the full picture.

If someone in your team has an idea and wants to do something with it, the answer is "let me get the Innovation Team involved" and not "I will knock something up tonight."

## What you can and cannot do yourself, day to day

**You can:**
- Use your firm's internal AI assistant for non-confidential tasks (drafting, summarising, brainstorming, learning)
- Use any firm-approved AI platform on matters where it has been cleared for that client
- Use Copilot for productivity tasks in M365 (drafting emails, summarising Teams threads, working in Word and Excel)
- Suggest ideas to your supervisor and to the Innovation Team
- Spot patterns in your team's work that look like prototype candidates

**You should not:**
- Build prototypes for live matters on personal devices
- Put confidential client data into tools that have not been approved for it
- Skip the firm's data classification process because "it is just a prototype"
- Promise a partner that you can build something for them this week (route it through the Innovation Team)

The AI Acceptable Use Policy and AI Governance Policy are the authoritative versions of this. Read them if you have not.

---

## How to bring an idea to the Innovation Team

If your team has an idea worth taking further, the route is:

1. Write a one-paragraph description: who is the user, what problem does it solve, what would the tool do, what data does it touch
2. Send it to the Innovation Team (any of the people who facilitated today, or your usual contact)
3. We will tell you whether it is something we can prototype, whether it is already covered by an existing tool, or whether it needs a different route

Do not worry about whether the idea is "big enough." Most of the highest-value tools we have built started as a one-line frustration from a trainee or associate.

## Three things worth doing this week

1. **Write down the three pain points your team complains about most.** Not the strategic ones. The boring, repetitive ones. The "we always have to redo this" ones.
2. **Mention the day in your next team meeting.** Two minutes, not ten. Use the 60-second version above.
3. **Pick one task you do this week and ask: could this have been a 90-minute prototype?** You do not need to build it. Just notice it.
