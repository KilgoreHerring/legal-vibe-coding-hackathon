# The Plan for the Day

Vibe Coding Hackathon - hand this out at the door.

You're in a group of around four or five, paired with one facilitator from the Innovation Team. In about an hour and a half your group will build a working internal app from scratch - no coding required from you - and a few groups will show what they built to the room.

*Times below are indicative. The exact running order will be on the screen on the day.*

---

## Running order (about 90 minutes)

| Time | What's happening |
|---|---|
| 0:00 | **Welcome** - the hosts: what vibe coding is, why we're doing this, how the session works (10 min) |
| 0:10 | **The build session - 60 minutes** (breakdown below) - at your table with your facilitator |
| 1:10 | **Present back** - three to five groups show what they built, ~4 minutes each (20 min) |
| 1:30 | Done |

---

## Inside the 60-minute build session

It moves quickly. Your facilitator keeps you to time.

| | What you're doing |
|---|---|
| First 10 min | **Discovery** - work out what you're building and who it's for. Be specific. If you're still debating after 10 minutes, pick one and go. |
| Next 5 min | **Design brief and kickoff** - turn the idea into a one-line plan, then your facilitator runs `/kickoff` and the build starts. |
| Next 35 min | **Build** - describe what you want, look at what comes back, say what's wrong, repeat. |
| Last 10 min | **Polish and lock the demo** - tighten it up, no new features, run `/present`, decide who says what. |

If your group already turns up with a clear idea, skip discovery and the design brief - describe it to Claude and start building straight away. Tell your facilitator.

Not every group presents back, so build something you'd be happy to show even if you're not picked - and be ready in case you are.

---

## Your job vs. the facilitator's job

**You:** come up with the idea, define the problem, describe what you want, test it, give feedback, tell the story if your group presents. The thinking is the work.

**Your facilitator:** types the prompts into Claude, handles any technical hiccups, keeps the build moving to time. You don't need to touch a keyboard unless you want to.

---

## What makes a good idea

Real - something you've actually experienced, not theoretical. Specific - "trainees waste time chasing signature pages" is a problem; "legal processes are inefficient" isn't. Contained - one focused tool, not a whole platform. With only an hour to build, lean smaller than feels comfortable.

Themes for today: **document tasks** (signature pages, redline summaries, CP lists) - **training and learning** (tools that help trainees get up to speed) - **relationships and BD** (staying close to clients, spotting opportunities) - **knowledge** (making the firm's expertise easier to find). You don't have to stay strictly within a theme.

Starting question: *"What's the most annoying thing I do repeatedly at work that a computer should probably be doing?"*

---

## If your group presents (about 4 minutes)

1. **The problem** - who has it and why it matters (30 sec)
2. **What you built** - one sentence, then a live demo - this is the centrepiece (2.5 min)
3. **What it would take to ship** and **the ask** - an honest line on what production would involve, and the next step if you took it further (1 min)

The presentation file generated during the session structures this for you.

---

## Don't worry about

- Whether you understand the code - you don't need to
- Whether your idea is too simple - simple and working beats complex and broken, especially in an hour
- Whether it would pass an IT review today - that's a known gap, not a failure; ask your facilitator about the path from prototype to pilot

## One thing to do before we start

Think about your last few weeks in your seat. What took longer than it should have? What did you do by hand that a computer could have handled? What did you have to chase down that should have been easy to find? That's probably your app idea.
